1. Storm deaths are a map problem, not a player problem
Storm deaths cluster in the same spots every match — western river corridor and southern edge on AmbroseValley. These zones barely show up on the kill heatmap, so players aren't getting caught in fights. The map just isn't giving them a way out when the storm pushes in.
Evidence: Consistent across all 5 days. Storm death and kill zone heatmaps show minimal overlap.
What to do: Widen exit routes from the two worst clusters. Add earlier environmental cues in those specific zones. Track storm death rate per zone — target 30% drop within 2 patches.

2. Winning the hardest fight on GrandRift leaves you worse off than avoiding it
Mine Pit is the hottest kill zone on GrandRift but has 2-3x fewer loot pickups than Labour Quarters or Engineer's Quarters. Players move through it fast — it's a fight zone, not a loot zone. So the players who win the toughest fights come out under equipped compared to someone who just quietly looted elsewhere.
Evidence: Kill heatmap vs loot heatmap comparison on GrandRift. Player paths show quick traversal through Mine Pit, not farming.
What to do: Either add loot inside Mine Pit to reward the risk, or add clear signposting toward Gas Station south for winners. Measure loot pickups per player near Mine Pit vs Labour Quarters — aim for parity within one patch.

3. Bot heavy matches will give you wrong conclusions about player behavior
On lighter days (Feb 13-14), several matches have 2-4 humans and 30+ bots. Bots follow scripted paths and don't behave like real players. If you're analyzing this data without filtering bots out, you'll draw incorrect conclusions about how humans actually navigate the map.
Evidence: Feb 14 timeline views show bot paths dominating with sparse human presence. Human UUID count per match drops significantly vs Feb 10-11.
What to do: Default the tool to human only view for analysis. Flag matches with fewer than 5 humans as bot majority and exclude from design decisions.
